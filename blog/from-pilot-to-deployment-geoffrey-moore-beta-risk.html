From Pilot to Deployment: Geoffrey Moore on the Risk SME Leaders Are Ignoring

The high-tech sector has already shown that AI can deliver massive ROI. Yet project after project languishes in pilot. Geoffrey Moore says the bottleneck is not technology. It is how we measure risk. For mid-market business leaders, that diagnosis changes everything.

Geoffrey Moore, the strategist behind Crossing the Chasm, recently made an argument that should land hard for anyone running a mid-market business: the AI adoption tornado is not being held back by capability. It is being held back by risk management that has lost its balance.

His framing is simple and uncomfortable. Organizations have spent years running AI pilots to reduce alpha risk, the downside of doing something new. What they have largely failed to do is confront beta risk, the downside of not doing it. The result is a landscape full of cautious experiments and almost no production deployments. For SME leaders who feel the wave passing them by while competitors talk about "AI strategy" in slide decks, Moore's analysis explains the stall. It also points toward a way out.

Two killer apps, and nothing else

Moore argues that AI's deployable value right now concentrates in two patterns, and that spreading attention across anything else is a mistake.

The first is expert amplification: specialized co-pilots that merge frontier language models with application-specific workflows and proprietary data. Think patient care coordination, wealth management, supply chain visibility, B2B sales cycles, legal research, and the other domains where human expertise is scarce, expensive, and hard to scale. The killer app is not a generic chatbot. It is a co-pilot that knows your workflows, reads your context, and makes your best people faster without replacing their judgment.

The second is self-service for high-volume transactions: agents embedded in systems of record, executing workflows that already have guardrails built in. Insurance claims, patient registration, tech support, maintenance requests, permitting, password resets. These are not experimental use cases. They are processes with decades of history, defined rules, and clear success criteria. Deploy an agent here and you get faster service, lower cost, 24/7 coverage, and fewer errors.

Both patterns are visible from where we sit. Both are exactly the kind of work SaaS Squash helps mid-market B2B organizations design, build, and own. The question Moore raises is not whether these applications exist. It is why so few of them make it out of pilot.

Alpha risk vs. beta risk

Moore borrows a concept from statistical testing and applies it to organizational decision-making. Alpha risk is the risk of commission: the harm that might come from taking action. Deploy an AI agent to handle customer inquiries and something could go wrong. A bad answer, a leaked data point, a process edge case nobody tested. Pilots exist to shrink that uncertainty.

Beta risk is the risk of omission: the harm that comes from not acting. Leaving your dog in the car on a hot day is alpha risk. Not rolling down the windows is beta risk. Each one is consequential. You need to manage both.

Moore's core claim is that AI programs have become excellent at managing alpha risk and almost blind to beta risk. Pilots generate confidence. They produce demos, slide decks, and risk assessments. But they cannot eliminate the residual alpha risk of going to production, because unknown unknowns always remain. So organizations hesitate. They crawl-walk-run. Or, more dysfunctionally, they postpone deployment indefinitely.

Meanwhile, beta risk accumulates in plain sight:

  • Reps spending three hours every week compiling an owner update that an agent could draft in minutes.
  • RFP responses that take two days because institutional knowledge lives in people's heads instead of a structured context network.
  • Account status pulled manually before every call, often outdated by the time the rep dials.
  • New hires shadowing for weeks because onboarding knowledge was never captured in a form agents can read.

None of these failures make headlines. They just cost margin, slow growth, and burn out the people who know your business best. That is beta risk in an industrial distributor. The same logic applies to any SME where expertise is stretched thin and manual process is the default.

Why service providers won't lead the charge

Moore makes a point that mid-market leaders should take personally: the organizations currently running your processes are deeply beholden to the status quo. Budget pressure keeps them focused on keeping today's operations alive, not funding tomorrow's disruption. You cannot expect your ERP vendor, your SaaS stack, or your IT service provider to lead a rebellion against the way things work now.

Adoption, Moore argues, must be driven by constituencies at both ends of the value chain: the people who receive the service and the people who pay for it. Until those groups conduct an explicit analysis of beta risk and accept responsibility for addressing it, nothing changes.

For an SME business leader, you often are that constituency. You are the economic buyer, the operator who feels the daily pain, and the person whose margin depends on whether your team spends its week on revenue work or administrative toil. Waiting for a vendor to solve this for you is itself a beta risk decision.

Moore's call to action: write the beta risk report

Moore's prescription is practical, not philosophical:

  1. Assemble a working team that represents the relevant stakeholders (operations, finance, IT, the people doing the manual work).
  2. Ask them to generate a beta risk report: a clear accounting of what it costs to keep doing things the current way.
  3. Balance that report against the alpha risk assessments you already have from pilots and governance reviews.
  4. Make the call. Is the status quo actually OK? Sometimes it is. But when it clearly is not, accept whatever alpha risk remains and reprioritize resources to fix the problem.

This is not a technology exercise. It is a leadership exercise. And it is one where the mid-market has an advantage over enterprise: you can assemble that working team in a single room, identify the three workflows where manual process is costing you the most, and move on them without eighteen months of committee review.

What SME leaders should do this quarter

Moore's article is aimed at the high-tech sector, but the logic applies with even more force to mid-market businesses that cannot afford to run infinite pilots while competitors ship.

  • Pick one of the two killer apps. Are you trying to amplify expertise (faster RFPs, smarter account prep, better owner updates) or automate high-volume transactions (order entry, CRM hygiene, customer intake)? Doing both at once is how pilots never end.
  • Write the beta risk report before you write another alpha risk assessment. Quantify what manual process is costing you this month in hours, errors, and missed revenue. That number is your case for deployment.
  • Build to own, not to rent. A pilot on someone else's platform produces a demo you do not control. A system your team builds and understands produces capability that compounds.
  • Measure training and consulting by what ships. If a session ends with understanding but nothing in production, you bought literacy, not leverage. Hold every AI investment to the standard Moore implies: did it move something out of pilot and into the business?

The AI wave Moore describes is not coming. It is here. The organizations catching it are not the ones with the most cautious pilots. They are the ones that looked honestly at beta risk, accepted the residual alpha risk of going live, and built systems their teams actually own.

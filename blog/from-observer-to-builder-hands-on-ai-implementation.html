AI Was Happening Around Us. Then We Built Something Real.

Most AI training ends at demos and slide decks. Here's what happened when one VP of Infrastructure stopped observing and started building — and why that changed the way his whole team talks about technology decisions.

Ask any SME leader what they know about AI and you'll usually get some version of the same answer: a lot, in theory. They've read the announcements, sat through vendor pitches, attended the webinars. They can name the tools. They understand, in broad strokes, that something significant is shifting.

And then ask what they've actually built, and the room goes quiet.

That gap — between knowing AI exists and knowing enough to make real decisions about it — is where most organizations spend the most time and where they're most vulnerable. Vendors fill the vacuum. Consultants sell the roadmap. The internal team nods along without enough foundation to push back intelligently. It's expensive to be an observer in a conversation your team needs you to lead.

The demo problem

Most AI training is built around demonstration. A vendor shows you what the platform can do. A workshop walks you through existing case studies. A course explains the concepts. All of it is useful, up to a point — but none of it leaves you with something you built.

The problem with demos is what they train you to do: watch. You leave with a better vocabulary and a clearer picture of the landscape, but you haven't touched the stack. You don't know what it felt like to make a decision halfway through a build and realize why it mattered. You can't evaluate a vendor's claim about their MCP integration because you've never built one.

For infrastructure leaders, that gap is particularly consequential. AI tooling decisions aren't abstract — they land in your stack, affect your team's workflow, and create maintenance burden or eliminate it. Making those decisions from a seat in the audience is a disadvantage.

What hands-on actually looks like

Greg Hantgin, VP of Infrastructure, came to SaaS Squash at a familiar inflection point: his organization was fielding AI questions from leadership and vendors, but he didn't yet have the foundation to evaluate them confidently.

The training didn't start with slides. It started with a build.

“Before working with SaaS Squash, AI felt like something happening around me rather than something we could actively start using. The training with Alex was hands-on from the start. We built a CRM database backend in Supabase, populated it with realistic mocked data, and then constructed an MCP server from scratch to actually converse with that data. It wasn’t a demo or a slide deck; it was a real implementation. That experience gave me the foundation to have much more informed conversations with my infrastructure team about where AI tooling fits into our stack. SaaS Squash made what felt like a complex, abstract concept into something tangible and immediately applicable.”

— Greg Hantgin, VP of Infrastructure

The session covered three concrete deliverables. First: a CRM database backend built in Supabase, not a toy schema but a structure realistic enough to reflect what an actual customer dataset looks like. Second: realistic mocked data to populate it — the kind of data that surfaces the real questions (what happens when fields are missing, when formats don't match, when queries need to span tables). Third: an MCP server built from scratch to converse with that data — a live, working interface that could answer questions about the database using natural language.

Each step was a real artifact. Not a screenshot of someone else's work. Something that ran.

Why the infrastructure conversation changes

The practical outcome of building something isn't just technical confidence — it's a different kind of participation in the conversations that follow.

When a vendor pitches an MCP integration, someone who has built an MCP server knows what questions to ask about the authorization model, the tool schema, and what happens at the edges. When a team proposes adding AI to a database workflow, a leader who has wired up Supabase to a language model understands the latency tradeoffs and the failure modes. When leadership asks "why can't we just use the AI for X," the answer isn't "I'm not sure" — it's grounded in what the stack can and can't do.

The shift Greg described — from AI feeling like something happening around him to having a foundation to engage his infrastructure team — is the exact shift that makes technology decisions better. Not because he became a developer, but because he became an informed participant in a conversation that was previously happening without him.

What SME leaders should take from this

The organizations that fall furthest behind on AI adoption aren't the ones that lack budget or ambition. They're the ones where decision-makers remain observers. Where the team that builds and the team that decides are not speaking the same language.

The answer isn't a longer course or a better vendor briefing. It's a real implementation session that produces something you can point to and say: I built that. I know what it does and why.

For SME leaders specifically, the goal doesn't need to be deep technical fluency. It's enough to understand the stack well enough to ask the right questions, evaluate claims honestly, and have a basis for the conversations your team is already trying to have with you.

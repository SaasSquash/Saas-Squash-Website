NFPA 70E and the New Electrical Safety PPE Hitting the Market

Arc-rated gear keeps getting lighter, stretchier, and easier to wear all day. That is good news for compliance. It does not replace the risk assessment NFPA 70E still requires before anyone steps inside an arc flash boundary.

If you manage electrical work at a utility, a plant, or any facility with energized equipment, NFPA 70E is the standard that tells you how to keep people alive around it. The current edition is NFPA 70E-2024, published in late 2023 and effective now. The next revision, NFPA 70E-2027, is in final committee review with issuance expected in fall 2026.

Between April and July 2026, a cluster of new arc-rated fabrics, garments, and gloves landed on the market. They are worth knowing about if you are refreshing a PPE program, but they sit at the bottom of a hierarchy that the 2024 standard made harder to skip.

What NFPA 70E actually requires before you buy gear

NFPA 70E is not a catalog of gloves and coveralls. It is a framework for deciding when energized work is permitted, how you assess the hazard, and what protection is required at a given incident energy level.

The 2024 edition strengthened a point that was always implied: your risk assessment must document the hierarchy of risk controls applied to each task. That hierarchy runs elimination first (de-energize and lock out), then substitution, engineering controls, awareness, administrative controls, and finally PPE. Selecting arc-rated clothing without documenting why higher-order controls were not feasible is non-compliant, not cautious.

Once you have done that analysis, PPE selection follows incident energy. NFPA 70E Table 130.5(C) maps exposure levels to PPE categories:

  • Category 1: minimum 4 cal/cm² arc rating
  • Category 2: minimum 8 cal/cm²
  • Category 3: minimum 25 cal/cm² (arc flash suit hood required above 12 cal/cm²)
  • Category 4: minimum 40 cal/cm²

Hand protection inside the arc flash boundary must be arc-rated gloves, heavy-duty leather, or rubber insulating gloves with leather protectors. Above Category 2, face shields alone are not enough; a hood is required. These rules have not changed because a new shirt feels softer. They still govern what you can assign to a task.

Employers must also audit their entire electrical safety program at least every three years, covering risk assessments, training, PPE use, and emergency response. If your program still references the 2021 edition, it needs a review.

Why the market is moving now

Electrical workers have tolerated stiff, heavy arc-rated rainwear for years because the alternative was worse. Suppliers are responding with fabrics that trade bulk for breathability without dropping below required ATPV ratings. Several launches in Q2 2026 converged around the same trade show cycle (Techtextil Frankfurt, April 21 to 24), which explains why so many announcements landed within weeks of each other.

None of this replaces your arc flash study or your energized work permit process. It does give safety managers more options when workers push back on gear they will not wear.

New PPE and fabric offerings from the last 90 days

Magid M-Defense FR (April 2026)

Magid launched its M-Defense FR Arc and Flash Fire Resistant Apparel line in early April, starting with the ARP450GN long-sleeve shirt and ARH650GN balaclava. Both use a blend of Lenzing FR viscose and Nomex. The line is UL certified to NFPA 2112 and compliant with NFPA 70E and ASTM F1506. Moisture-wicking construction and UPF 50+ sun protection target outdoor utility and industrial crews who need Category-level body coverage without flash-fire-only gear that ignores arc hazards.

Lenzing Solutions for Protective Wear (April 14, 2026)

Lenzing introduced a three-tier protective wear portfolio anchored by LENZING FR fibers for inherent flame resistance, with hybrid and FR-finished tiers for lower-risk environments. The inherent tier targets electrical utilities alongside structural firefighting. Lenzing cited independent testing at 35°C showing a 29% lower rise in core body temperature versus FR-treated cotton when wearing LENZING FR/aramid blends. For specifiers, the value is a single fiber supplier across tiers rather than mixing incompatible fabric systems across a fleet uniform program.

Prolen Core technical yarn (May 8, 2026)

Filidea Technical Yarns, Waxman, and Lenzing debuted Prolen Core at Techtextil 2026. The yarn combines Waxman's Protex F modacrylic with dope-dyed LENZING FR. Lab tests on fabrics built from the blend showed improved arc resistance versus prior generations, and the antimony-free Protex F formulation aligns with tightening environmental standards for flame-retardant chemistry. This is a supply-chain play: garment makers get a pre-engineered yarn rather than sourcing and blending components themselves.

Textilcolor pyroshell (April 2026, Techtextil debut)

Swiss specialist Textilcolor presented pyroshell, an expandable-graphite flame protection technology for synthetic textiles. The company positions it for heat, flame, and electric arc applications without halogen or phosphorus additives, which matters for toxic fume concerns during a garment failure event. pyroshell stays lightweight and breathable relative to traditional FR coatings, and can be combined with electrostatic dissipation or chemical splash resistance depending on fabric construction.

3H Safety PFA-DJ3 Twill FR fabric (May 20, 2026)

3H Safety Technology released the PFA-DJ3 Twill Electric Arc Protection FR Fabric, a 230 gsm modacrylic-aramid twill with permanent flame retardancy, antistatic fibers, and arc protection for substation, rail, and heavy equipment maintenance work. This is fabric-level supply for OEMs and uniform programs rather than a finished garment, but it signals continued investment in arc-rated textiles from Asian manufacturers serving global utility markets.

NM Safety NFPA 70E arc flash gloves (April 14, 2026)

NM Safety expanded its arc flash glove line with NFPA 70E-compliant options rated at 10 cal/cm² ATPV (Category 2 territory), ANSI cut level A4, and a 13-gauge liner for dexterity. Hand injuries in arc events are common when workers downgrade to general-purpose gloves inside the boundary. A dedicated arc-rated glove at this rating fills the gap between heavy leather and full rubber insulating systems for tasks that need fine motor control.

ProGARM Gen 2 and GORE-TEX PYRAD stretch (preorders through summer 2026)

UK arc flash specialist ProGARM opened preorders on its Gen 2 range and its collaboration with GORE-TEX Professional using arc-rated GORE-TEX PYRAD stretch technology. Gore reports the stretch fabric delivers IEC 61482-1-2 Class 2 protection with an ATPV up to 46 cal/cm², roughly 45% lighter than comparable Class 2 rainwear, with 50% more stretch than existing arc-rated hardshells. Jackets, trousers, and coveralls target foul-weather utility work where workers historically chose non-compliant rain gear over bulky arc suits. Warehouse stock was scheduled for July 2026.

What to do with this information

New product announcements are not a reason to re-specify your entire program overnight. They are a reason to ask better questions during your next three-year audit:

  • Does our risk assessment document why PPE is the control we rely on for each energized task?
  • Are arc flash labels current, and do they match the PPE categories we actually stock?
  • Do workers refuse specific garments because of weight, heat stress, or fit? If so, lighter fabrics and stretch foul-weather gear may improve compliance without lowering ratings.
  • Are hand protection choices mapped to incident energy, not habit?
  • When NFPA 70E-2027 publishes this fall, which gaps in our program will the new edition expose?

PPE is the last line of defense. The 2024 standard says so explicitly. But the last line only works if people wear it, and the gear arriving this quarter makes that easier than it was a year ago.
